Introduction

Protected areas or conservation areas are locations which receive protection because of their recognized natural, ecological, or cultural values. Protected areas are those areas in which human presence or the exploitation of natural resources (e.g., firewood, non-timber forest products, water, ...) is limited.

The term "protected area" also includes marine protected areas and transboundary protected areas across multiple borders. As of 2016, there are over 161,000 protected areas representing about 17 percent of the world's land surface area (excluding Antarctica).

Protected Areas and Climate Change

Protected Areas alleviate climate change effects in a variety of ways:

  • Prohibition of degradative activities including: fishing, hunting, development, agriculture, mining, logging, and sometimes entry.
  • Reduction in: shifts in species distribution, intensity of storms, and impacts of sea level rise.
  • Climate change threats to coral reefs
  • Combats ocean acidification by: maintaining coastal primary producers, increasing teleost biomass (osmoregulation buffers pH change), and increasing natural carbon sequestration via the biological pump
  • Increased: populations, species resilience, genetic diversity, and potential for adaptations, lowering the risk of extinction

Importance of Protected Areas

  1. Biodiversity Conservation: Safeguarding habitats and endangered species.
  2. Ecosystem Services: Purifying water, regulating climate.
  3. Human Well-being: Providing recreation, supporting cultural values.
  4. Climate Change: Mitigating through carbon sequestration.
  5. Scientific Research: Serving as vital research and monitoring sites.
  6. Sustainable Resource Management: Regulating resource extraction sustainably.
  7. Preserving Landscapes: Maintaining natural beauty and cultural significance.
  8. Global Connectivity: Facilitating wildlife movement and genetic diversity.
